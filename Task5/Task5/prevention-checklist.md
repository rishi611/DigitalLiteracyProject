# Stay Safe Online – Prevention Checklist
### Task 5 – Cybercrime Awareness | CSE0001 Digital Literacy

A practical guide for college students in India to stay protected from online threats.

---

## General Online Safety

- [ ] **Every account deserves its own password** — and not a weak one. Aim for at least 8 characters with a mix of cases, numbers, and symbols. Reusing passwords across accounts is one of the easiest ways to get compromised. A password manager makes this way less of a headache.

- [ ] **Switch on Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) wherever you can** — especially for email, payment apps, and social media. Even if your password ends up in the wrong hands, 2FA means the attacker still can't get in without that second step.

- [ ] **Treat every unexpected link with suspicion** — whether it came through SMS, WhatsApp, email, or a DM. It doesn't matter if the sender looks familiar. Always check directly with that person through another channel before clicking anything.

- [ ] **Don't delay software and app updates.** Security patches are released for a reason — there are known vulnerabilities being actively exploited, and updates close those gaps.

- [ ] **Public Wi-Fi and sensitive accounts don't mix well.** Avoid logging into your bank, making payments, or accessing personal accounts on public networks. If you have no other option, use a VPN to add a layer of protection.

- [ ] **Go through your app permissions once in a while.** A flashlight app doesn't need your contacts. A food delivery app doesn't need your microphone. Revoke access that doesn't make sense.

---

## UPI and Financial Safety 💳

- [ ] **Your UPI PIN and OTP are yours alone — no exceptions.** It doesn't matter if the caller says they're from your bank, NPCI, or even a government office. No legitimate organisation will ever ask for these details. Hanging up is always the right move.

- [ ] **Entering your PIN is a sending action, not a receiving one.** This is the most common misconception scammers exploit. On UPI, you enter your PIN only when money is going out of your account. If someone says you need it to receive a payment, walk away — it's a trap.

- [ ] **QR codes from unknown sources are a red flag.** In online buy-sell situations especially, scammers send QR codes claiming it'll help you receive money. In reality, scanning it initiates a debit from your account. Never scan a QR code you didn't ask for.

- [ ] **Cap your daily UPI limit as a safety measure.** Most payment apps let you set this. It won't stop fraud, but it puts a ceiling on how much damage can happen before you notice and act.

---

## Social Media and Identity Safety

- [ ] **Keep documents off social media entirely.** Your Aadhaar, PAN, college ID, hall ticket, or even a travel booking — posting these, even for a second, is a risk you don't need to take. The information on them can be misused in ways you won't see coming.

- [ ] **Audit your profile visibility every few months.** Check who can actually see your phone number, email, and location. Platforms change their defaults, and what was private before might not be anymore.

---

## If You Are Targeted

- [ ] **Time matters — call your bank immediately.** If you suspect your account has been accessed without your knowledge, call the 24x7 helpline right away and request a freeze. Every minute counts with UPI fraud.

- [ ] **File a complaint at [cybercrime.gov.in](https://cybercrime.gov.in)** — India's official portal for reporting online crimes, including financial fraud.

- [ ] **Dial 1930 for the National Cyber Crime Helpline.** It's free, available round the clock, and specifically set up to assist with cases like UPI and online fraud.

- [ ] **Document everything before reporting.** Screenshots of messages, call logs, transaction IDs — gather all of it. This becomes your evidence and makes the complaint process a lot smoother.

---

> **Remember:** Staying cautious online isn't overcautious — it's just smart. If something feels even slightly off, pause. Verify. Then decide.
